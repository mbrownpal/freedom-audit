# Freedom Audit - Realistic Test Answers
## Profile: $5M+ Founder

**Name:** Alex Chen
**Email:** test@example.com
**Profile:** 42, sold SaaS company 2 years ago for $8M, now running portfolio of 3 ventures

---

## Question 1: Where You Are Now - Current Season

I'm in what I'd call the "post-exit recalibration" phase. Sold my marketing automation company two years ago after building it for nine years. The sale was good—$8M with earnout that actually paid—but the transition was harder than I expected. I went from running a 40-person team and being needed constantly to suddenly having capital but no clear next thing. I've since started three different ventures: a coaching practice for B2B founders, a small fund investing in SaaS tools, and I'm advising two portfolio companies. On paper it looks great. In reality, I'm busier than when I had the company, making less money than I did at exit, and I can't tell if I'm building something real or just staying busy because I don't know how to not be busy. My wife says I'm running from something. She's probably right, but I haven't stopped long enough to figure out what.

## Question 2: Where You Are Now - 12 Months From Now

If this goes really well, I've made an actual decision about what I'm building instead of hedging across three half-committed things. The coaching practice either scales to something real—maybe 20 clients at $30K each with a waitlist—or I shut it down and admit it was never the thing. The fund either raises a proper Fund II with committed LPs, or I acknowledge I was playing venture capitalist and return to operating. Most importantly, I'm not waking up at 3am anymore with this low-grade anxiety that I'm wasting the exit window. I want to be present with my kids—they're 8 and 11, and I'm missing it. I want my marriage to feel like partnership again instead of roommates managing logistics. Financially, I want $250K in annual passive income locked in so I can take real risk with my time without the background hum of scarcity. And honestly, I want to know what I'm actually good at versus what I was just good at because I had to be.

## Question 3: Freedom of Health - Rating

I'd give it a 6.5. The good: I'm not actively unhealthy. I work out 3-4 times a week, I've got a trainer, my bloodwork is fine, I'm not on any medications. I sleep 6-7 hours most nights which isn't terrible. I eat reasonably well when I'm home. The not good: I'm 20 pounds heavier than I was at exit, and I can feel it. I drink more than I should—two glasses of wine most nights, sometimes three. It's not a problem problem, but it's a thing. I'm always slightly tired, and I've stopped doing the things that actually made me feel good: morning walks, stretching, the sauna routine I had for a while. I travel constantly for the fund stuff, which wrecks any routine. My trainer says my body is fine but my nervous system is fried. That tracks. I'm not in crisis, but I'm coasting on a younger version of myself's foundation, and I can feel it eroding. I know what I need to do—I just haven't prioritized it over everything else screaming for attention.

## Question 4: Freedom of Relationships - Rating & Details

Honestly, a 7, maybe 7.5 on good weeks. My marriage is solid but strained. We've been together 15 years, and we've built a good life—two great kids, financial security, shared values. But somewhere in the last few years of the company and the chaos after exit, we stopped being partners and became project managers. We talk about schedules, kid logistics, bills, who's picking up what. We don't fight much, but we also don't connect much. She's patient, but I can feel her waiting for me to land somewhere. My kids are great, but I'm not as present as I want to be. I coach their sports, I show up to games, but I'm distracted. They know it. My closest friends are mostly founder friends, and those relationships are great—we get each other, we support each other, we have real conversations. But they're also competitive and transactional in ways I don't always love. I've got a few non-founder friends from college who feel more real, but I don't make time for them. Family is complicated—my parents are aging, my dad has health issues, and I'm the oldest so it falls to me. That relationship is more duty than connection. Broader community: I don't really have one. I used to be deeply involved in a church, and I miss that, but I haven't found it again.

## Question 5: Freedom of Relationships - Authenticity

I feel most free to be myself with my founder friends who've also exited. There's no pretense there—we all know the terror of payroll, the weirdness of sudden wealth, the identity crisis that comes after you sell. We talk about the real stuff: therapy, marriage strain, what the hell we're supposed to do now. I also feel fully myself with my kids when I'm actually present—they don't care about my resume, they just want me to play. With my wife, it's complicated. I want to be fully myself, but I'm also managing her anxiety about what's next. With clients and LPs, I'm definitely performing—I'm the successful founder who figured it out, even though half the time I feel like I'm making it up. I'm still wearing the "guy who has his shit together" mask in most professional settings, and honestly in most social settings too. My parents have never really understood what I do, so with them I'm the dutiful son who's "doing well." The version I'm wearing that's least true is probably the "I've got it all figured out" persona I default to in new professional relationships. I hate that version of myself, but I keep doing it.

## Question 6: Freedom of Time - Rating & How Time is Spent

I'd say a 5, maybe 5.5. Here's what my actual days look like: I wake up around 6:30, check email and Slack before I even get out of bed (I know, I know). I try to work out 7-8am but it happens maybe 3 times a week. By 9am I'm in meetings—coaching calls, fund due diligence, advisor check-ins, podcast interviews. Lunch is usually at my desk or a "networking lunch" that's really just work. Afternoons are more meetings, writing (investor updates, thought leadership, emails), and putting out fires in the portfolio companies. I'm supposed to end by 6 to do family dinner, and I do maybe 4 nights a week. After kids are in bed I'm back online—investor emails, reading pitch decks, catching up on what I missed. I'd say 60% of my time is reactive—responding to other people's agendas. 30% is chosen but not strategic, just busy work that feels productive. Maybe 10% is actually building something that matters. The biggest issue: I have no white space. I've optimized my calendar to the point where there's no room to think. I tell myself I'll take a week off to plan, but it never happens. Travel eats up at least one week a month, which destroys any routine. The trade-offs I'm making aren't really conscious—I'm just saying yes to things and hoping it works out.

## Question 7: Freedom of Mind - Mental Clarity Rating

I'd give it a 6, some days a 7. On good days—usually after a workout and when I have a clear block of deep work time—my mind feels sharp, focused, energizing. I'm solving problems, connecting dots, feeling like I'm building something. Those days are great. Most days, though, it's more like a 5 or 6. My mind is constantly ping-ponging between contexts: fund stuff, coaching, advising, family logistics, random fires. It's exhausting. I feel most like myself early mornings before the day's chaos starts, or late at night when everything is quiet and I can finally think. But I'm often too tired by then to do anything with it. My work is a mix: the coaching is energizing when I'm with the right clients, draining when I'm not. The fund stuff is intellectually stimulating but emotionally exhausting—so much ego management, so much performance. Advising can be really rewarding when I actually help someone avoid a mistake I made, but it can also feel like I'm LARPing as a sage when I'm still figuring my own stuff out. The common thread on draining days: I'm doing work that's more about maintaining an image than actually creating value. The energizing work is when I'm in real problem-solving mode with smart people who don't need me to perform.

## Question 8: Freedom of Mind - Outdated Beliefs

"More is better." I've built my entire life around accumulation—more revenue, more clients, more portfolio companies, more optionality. And I'm starting to realize that's a treadmill with no exit. I don't actually want more. I want the right thing. But I don't know how to operate in a world where I'm not optimizing for growth. "I'm only valuable when I'm producing." This one runs deep. My self-worth is tied to output. If I'm not shipping, building, closing deals, advising, writing, I feel like I'm failing. Rest feels like failure. White space feels like laziness. I know intellectually this is broken, but I can't seem to internalize it. "The market will tell me what to do." I've spent my whole career being customer-obsessed and market-driven, and it worked. But now I'm realizing the market doesn't care about my fulfillment, my family, or my mental health. The market will gladly consume me if I let it. I'm starting to suspect that the next phase requires leading with intention instead of reacting to opportunity. But that's terrifying because I don't trust my own compass the way I trust market feedback.

## Question 9: Freedom of Soul - Connection to Purpose

I'd give it a 5, maybe 5.5 on reflective days. Here's the truth: I used to feel deeply connected to something bigger. In my twenties, I was involved in a faith community, I volunteered, I felt part of something meaningful. That faded as the startup consumed everything. Now I'm not sure what I believe. I'm not religious in any formal sense anymore, but I'm also not not spiritual—I meditate occasionally, I read philosophy, I think about legacy and meaning. The work I'm doing now doesn't feel aligned with a deeper purpose. The coaching sometimes does—when I help a founder avoid burnout or make a hard decision, that feels meaningful. But a lot of it feels like I'm monetizing my exit story. The fund work is intellectually interesting but spiritually empty. Writing and teaching feel closer to purpose, but I don't make time for it because it doesn't pay the bills. If I'm honest, I'm in a meaning vacuum right now. I sold the company that gave me identity for a decade, and I haven't found the next thing that matters beyond making money and looking successful. My wife asked me last year what I'm building toward, and I didn't have an answer. That's the work I need to do. What does enough look like? What am I here to do that isn't just resume-building? I don't know yet.

## Question 10: Financial Foundation - Rating

I'd give it an 8, maybe 8.5. The good: I'm financially secure in ways I never imagined growing up. We have about $6M liquid after taxes from the exit, another $1.5M in the fund, $500K in retirement accounts, and our house is paid off (worth maybe $1.2M). We're generating about $180K a year in passive income from investments, and my coaching/advising brings in another $200K. My wife makes $80K from her part-time work. So we're at $460K a year, and we spend about $220K. We're saving aggressively, we're over-insured, we've got estate planning dialed. We could stop working tomorrow and be fine. The not-as-good: Despite all that security, I still operate from scarcity. I worry about money even though objectively I don't need to. I'm afraid to spend on things that might actually improve our quality of life because what if we need it later. I undercharge for coaching because I'm afraid people will say no. I took a lower carry on the fund because I was afraid LPs would walk. I know this is psychological, not financial, but it's real. Also, I'm working way too hard for the incremental dollars I'm earning. The delta between $460K and $600K wouldn't change our life, but I'm grinding for it anyway. That doesn't make sense, but I can't seem to stop.

## Question 11: Financial Foundation - What is "Enough"?

I've actually done this exercise, which is progress. Here's the math: We need $220K a year to live our current life comfortably. Add $40K for margin and lifestyle creep, so $260K. Assuming a 4% safe withdrawal rate, that's $6.5M in liquid assets. We're basically there. If I add in what I want—not need—it's closer to $350K a year. That's $8.75M. So realistically, if I hit $9M liquid and $350K in passive income, I could do whatever I want with my time without financial pressure. By that math, I'm 1-2 years away if I stay disciplined. But here's the thing: I don't trust the number. What if markets crash? What if one of the kids has massive medical expenses? What if I want to do something capital-intensive later? So I keep moving the goalpost. Three years ago "enough" was $5M. Now it's $9M. In two years it'll probably be $12M. I know intellectually that this is the hedonic treadmill, but emotionally I can't seem to lock in a number and stop. My therapist says this is about control—if I keep chasing more, I don't have to face the question of what I'd do if I actually had enough. She's probably right. That question terrifies me more than scarcity does.

## Question 12: Inner Landscape - Processing Stress and Pain

When something stressful happens, my first reaction is to fix it. Immediately. I go into problem-solving mode, start making lists, firing off emails, taking action. It feels productive, but I'm realizing it's often just avoiding the feeling. The stress sits in my chest—tight, constricted, like I can't get a full breath. I don't cry, I don't yell, I just clench. How long it sticks around depends on whether I can solve it. If it's something I can control, I fix it and move on in a few hours or days. If it's something outside my control—like my dad's health declining, or a portfolio company imploding despite my advice—it sits there for weeks. I'm not good at sitting with discomfort. I either work through it, work out to burn it off, or drink to numb it. My wife processes by talking, and she's tried to get me to do that, but I usually don't have words for it until it's already passed. Therapy has helped a little—I'm better at noticing the physical response now, and sometimes I can just let it be there without needing to fix it. But that's still new and uncomfortable for me. I move through pain by either solving the problem or outlasting it. Not super healthy, I know.

## Question 13: Inner Landscape - Relating to Desire

When I want something I don't have, it becomes a fixation. I zero in on it, strategize how to get it, and pursue it relentlessly until I have it or I realize it's impossible. It drives me in the sense that it gives me focus and energy. I'm really good at reverse-engineering goals and executing toward them. But it also distracts me—I can become so focused on the thing I want that I lose perspective on whether I actually need it or whether it's just the next dopamine hit. Example: I wanted a specific LP in the fund so badly that I spent three months courting them, and when they finally committed, the high lasted about 48 hours before I moved on to the next thing. That pattern repeats constantly. I'm also noticing that the desire is often about validation more than the actual thing. I wanted that LP because having them would prove I belonged in this world. I wanted to hit $10M net worth because it felt like a milestone that would make me feel secure. But the goalposts keep moving, and the security never comes. I think the healthy version of desire is letting it motivate me without letting it consume me. But I'm not there yet. Right now, desire drives me, but it also runs me.

## Question 14: Inner Landscape - Presence vs Autopilot

Honestly? I'd say 20-30% present, 70-80% autopilot or future-focused. I'm rarely fully in the moment. Even when I'm "relaxing"—playing with my kids, having dinner with my wife—part of my brain is running through tomorrow's to-do list, replaying a conversation from earlier, or strategizing the next move. My mind is almost always three steps ahead, which was great for building a company but is terrible for actually living. The times I'm genuinely present: deep in a coaching conversation where I'm fully locked in. Occasionally during workouts, especially when I'm really pushing. Sometimes when I'm reading something that fully captures my attention. And weirdly, when I'm doing something manual and physical—like building something or cooking a complex meal—I can get into flow. But most of my day? I'm running on autopilot. Morning routine, email, meetings, tasks, emails, meetings, dinner, kids, bed, repeat. I optimize everything for efficiency, which means I'm never actually experiencing it. I'm always in execution mode. The irony is I've built a life that's objectively pretty great, but I'm not actually living it—I'm just managing it.

## Question 15: Inner Landscape - The Undercurrent

When everything gets quiet and I'm really honest with myself, the undercurrent is fear. Fear that I peaked. Fear that the exit was a fluke and I'll never build something that meaningful again. Fear that I'm not actually that good, I just got lucky with timing and market. Fear that I'm wasting the window—I've got capital and credibility right now, but both have a shelf life, and I'm frittering them away on half-commitments instead of making a real bet. There's also this low-grade sense of fraudulence. People treat me like I've figured it out, but I feel like I'm making it up as I go. And underneath all of that is a quieter, scarier question: What if I get everything I'm chasing and it still doesn't feel like enough? What if the problem isn't the goals, it's me? What if I'm fundamentally not wired for contentment? I don't talk about this much because it feels ungrateful—I have more than I ever imagined, and here I am anxious about it. But when it's really quiet, that's what's running in the background. The sense that I'm running from something I haven't named yet, and no amount of achievement is going to outrun it.

## Question 16: Where It Comes From - Father's Relationship

My dad was a high school teacher. Stable, secure, respected in the community, but never made much money. He worked hard—coached sports, ran summer school, took on extra shifts—but he was always stressed about money. I remember him sitting at the kitchen table with bills, looking worried. He was proud of what he did, but there was also this sense of quiet resignation—like he'd settled for safety instead of chasing something bigger. He never said it, but I felt it: he wanted more, but he was afraid to risk it. How does that show up in me? I'm terrified of being stuck the way he was. That fear has driven everything—I will never be in a position where I can't provide, where I'm dependent on someone else, where I have to settle. But it's also made me unable to ever feel secure. No matter how much I have, it's never enough, because in the back of my mind is that image of my dad stressed about bills. The other thing I got from him: work is how you prove your worth. He taught me that respect comes from showing up, grinding, being reliable. That's served me well, but it's also why I can't stop working even when I don't need to. Rest feels like failure. Leisure feels like laziness. I inherited his work ethic, but I also inherited his anxiety. I swore I'd never be in his position financially, and I'm not, but I still carry his fear.

## Question 17: Where It Comes From - Mother's Relationship

My mom was a stay-at-home mom who went back to work part-time when I was in middle school. She was the emotional anchor of the family—she kept everything running, managed everyone's feelings, made sure we were okay. But she also sacrificed everything. She had dreams of being an artist, but she put them aside to raise us and support my dad. I don't think she resented it, exactly, but there was a wistfulness there. She'd sometimes say things like "maybe when you kids are grown" or "in another life," and I knew she'd given up parts of herself. How does that show up in me? I'm terrified of sacrificing my potential the way she did. I don't want to look back at 65 and wonder what I could have been if I'd just gone for it. That fear drives me to keep pushing, keep building, keep pursuing. But it's also made me selfish. I prioritize my work over my family in ways I don't love, because I'm so afraid of waking up with regret. My wife has called me on this—she's said I'm so focused on not becoming my dad that I'm not being present for her and the kids. She's right. The other thing I got from my mom: relationships are work, and you have to keep everyone else okay. I'm a chronic people-pleaser, I manage everyone's emotions, and I have a hard time setting boundaries. I picked that up from watching her. It's made me a good coach and a good advisor, but it's also exhausting. I'm still learning how to not carry everyone else's feelings.

## Question 18: Where It Comes From - Pattern That Won't Shake

I overcommit and then resent it. Every. Single. Time. I say yes to things I don't have capacity for because I'm afraid of disappointing people or missing an opportunity. Then I'm overwhelmed, resentful, and doing subpar work because I'm stretched too thin. I know this pattern. I've known it for years. I've been to therapy about it. I've read the books. I've set boundaries. And I still do it. What keeps it in place? Part of it is FOMO—what if this is the opportunity that changes everything? Part of it is people-pleasing—I don't want to let people down. Part of it is identity—"the guy who can handle anything" is part of how I see myself, and saying no feels like admitting I can't. But the deeper thing is this: if I say no and create space, then I have to face the uncomfortable question of what I actually want to do with that space. As long as I'm overcommitted, I have an excuse for not going deep on the things that actually matter. I can tell myself "I'd write that book if I had time" or "I'd be more present with my family if I wasn't so busy." But if I create the space and still don't do it, then I have to confront the possibility that I'm afraid of what matters most. So I stay busy. It's safer.

## Question 19: Where It Comes From - Family Role

I was the responsible one. The oldest kid, the one who got good grades, didn't cause problems, took care of my younger siblings when needed. I was the success story—first to go to college, first to build a career, first to "make it." My parents were proud of me, but there was also pressure in that. I was supposed to be the one who figured it out, who didn't need help, who made smart decisions. I was never allowed to be messy or uncertain. Am I still playing it? Absolutely. I'm the one my family calls when something needs to be solved. I'm the one who's supposed to have it together financially, emotionally, professionally. I'm the one who doesn't ask for help because that would disrupt the role. It shows up in my business life too—I'm the advisor, the coach, the person who helps other people figure it out. I'm rarely the one being vulnerable or asking for help. That role has served me well in a lot of ways, but it's also isolating. I'm so used to being the competent one that I don't know how to not be that. And it's exhausting. I want to be the person who doesn't have it figured out sometimes, who's allowed to be uncertain or struggling. But I don't know how to do that without feeling like I'm failing the role I've been playing my whole life.

## Question 20: Radical Honesty - Pretending Not to Know

I'm pretending not to know that I need to stop hedging and make a real decision about what I'm building. I have three different ventures running right now, and I tell myself I'm keeping optionality open, but the truth is I'm afraid to commit. I know the coaching practice is the thing that's most aligned with who I am, but it's also the one with the least financial upside and the most vulnerability required. So I keep the fund and the advising as backup plans. I'm also pretending not to know that my marriage needs more than I'm giving it. My wife has been patient, but I can feel the distance growing, and I'm hoping it will just fix itself once I "figure out what's next." It won't. I know that. But if I admit it, I have to do something about it, and I don't know if I have the emotional bandwidth right now. And I'm pretending not to know that the version of success I'm chasing isn't what I actually want. I'm still optimizing for external validation—investor interest, client testimonials, LinkedIn engagement—because it's measurable and it feels like progress. But I know deep down that's not going to make me feel fulfilled. I just don't know what will, so I keep chasing the thing I know how to measure.

## Question 21: Radical Honesty - Fear of Loss

If I actually got everything I say I want—financial freedom, time with family, work that matters, deep relationships, peace of mind—I'm most afraid of losing my drive. I'm afraid that if I stop chasing, I'll go soft. I'll lose the edge that made me successful. I'm afraid I'll become complacent, comfortable, and irrelevant. That the hunger that built the company was the thing that made me interesting, and without it I'm just another retired founder playing golf. I'm also afraid of losing the identity that comes with striving. "The guy who's building something" is who I am. If I'm not building, who am I? Just a guy with money? That feels empty. And there's a quieter fear: if I actually let myself be happy, something will be taken away. I don't trust good things to last. I grew up with enough financial stress that security still feels fragile, even when it's not. So part of me thinks if I relax and enjoy it, the other shoe will drop. My therapist says I'm afraid that if I'm not in control, chaos will take over. That tracks. But it's also exhausting. I want to believe that rest isn't giving up, that contentment isn't complacency, that I can be enough without constantly proving it. But I'm not there yet.

## Question 22: Radical Honesty - The Next Ten Years

If money, status, and other people's opinions were completely off the table, I'd write. I'd teach. I'd work with a small number of founders I genuinely care about and help them build companies and lives they're proud of. I'd spend way more time with my kids while they still want to hang out with me. I'd take my wife on trips where we're actually present with each other instead of just managing logistics. I'd get back into a faith community or a philosophical practice that gives me a sense of meaning beyond work. I'd probably build something small and focused—maybe a cohort-based program, maybe a tiny fund with 5 LPs I actually like—but it would be about impact, not scale. I'd read more, write more, walk more, think more. I'd have white space in my calendar. I'd stop performing and just exist. I'd probably make way less money, and I'd be fine with it. The life I'm describing is possible right now. I don't need anyone's permission. I don't need more capital. I just need to decide it's okay. That's the terrifying part. If I can have that life and I'm choosing not to, then I have to admit I'm the thing in my own way. Not the market, not my obligations, not external pressure. Just me. And that's the work.
